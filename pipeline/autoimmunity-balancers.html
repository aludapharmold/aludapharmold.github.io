Autoimmunity Th17/Treg Balancers

Psoriasis is a chronic inflammatory skin disease affecting approximately 2 - 4% of the population in western countries and, despite recent advances, remains an important unmet medical need. Epidermal hyperplasia, vascular proliferation and leukocyte infiltration are the main characteristics of psoriatic skin. Keratinocyte hyper-proliferation is driven by inflammatory cytokines, such as IL12, IL23, IL17, IFN-gamma, TNF and IL-22, which are secreted by activated resident T cells, dendritic cells and other innate immune cells, as well as the keratinocytes themselves. The interplay between auto-reactive immune cells and activated keratinocytes forms a self-perpetuating autoimmune cycle, resulting in an amplified and sustained inflammation, and consequently the recurrent skin lesions present in psoriasis patients.

During the past decade, it has become increasingly recognized that psoriasis is mediated by auto-reactive resident memory T cells in the skin, with many innate and adaptive immune cells contributing to the pathological processes. Resident memory T cells are non-recirculating T cells in the epithelial barrier that can recognize a wide variety of antigens. They respond to pathogen challenge independently of recruitment of T cells from the blood and provide rapid and highly effective in situ protective immunity under physiological conditions. When over-responding to the challenge, they cause tissue damage leading to the clinical symptoms of psoriasis.

Suppressing T cell-mediated autoimmunity and blocking cytokine-mediated inflammation represent two effective approaches in developing anti-psoriatic medicine. The T cell-suppressive medication cyclosporine can alleviate psoriatic symptoms. Biologics targeting cytokines, such as TNFa blockers (etanercept, adalimumab, infliximab, golimumab, certolizumab pegol), and the anti-IL12/23 antibody ustekinumab, help to stop the inflammatory cycle of psoriatic disease. The anti-IL17A therapy secukinumab has recently demonstrated remarkable efficacy, suggesting the dominant roles of IL17 signaling and Th17 mediated autoimmunity in the pathogenesis of psoriasis.

STAT3 is critical in Th17 cell differentiation and Th17-mediated autoimmunity. Genome-wide association studies have implicated STAT3 signaling pathway in the development of many diseases, such as IBD, lupus and psoriasis. In addition to its effects on immune components, constitutive STAT3 activity in keratinocytes leads to a skin phenotype of psoriasis in mouse models, suggesting that STAT3 links activated keratinocytes and infiltrated leukocytes in psoriasis development.

Due to extensive research publications on their oncogenic role in cancer cells, STAT3 inhibitors have mainly been evaluated for cancer therapy. They have not, however, proven effective in cancer patients but have served to expand our understanding. Also informative is the clinical success of combined JAK-STAT3 inhibitors in immunological applications. Examples are the IL6 receptor antibody tocilizumab and the JAK inhibitor tofacitinib. Based on the key roles of STAT3 in the immune system and the clinical success of inhibiting the components of JAK-STAT pathway upstream of STAT3, Aluda investigated STAT3 Y705p inhibition as an effective strategy to treat autoimmune diseases and has a series of compounds in early development.
